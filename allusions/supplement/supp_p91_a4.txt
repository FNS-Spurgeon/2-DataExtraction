1882. Mitchell, James, Letter to Dr. Furnivall, April 3rd.
SIR,
In answer to your query of yesterday morning :—The
walls of Blenheim Park are built of stone dug on the estate,—
a kind of light brown oolite, certainly not green stone. They
have in places grown green with age & moss. Chaucer uses a
poet’s licence in describing them as green. The Palace itself
is built of Taynton stone (oolite). Do not know of any green
stone to be found in this neighbourhood.
[The reference here is to the Parlement of Foules, l. 123:
This forseid African me hente anoon,
And forth with him unto a gate broghte
Right of a parke, walled with grene stoon.
Hales, in Gentleman's Magazine, showed there was a park at
Woodstock surrounded by a stone wall, and it has been held
that Chaucer was thinking of this in the passage above.
Köppel, however, in Anglia, xiv. 234, has shown that here the
poet partly follows Boccaccio’s Amorosa Visione, where there
is mention of un muro antico. (See Skeat’s note to the P. of
Foules, Library edn., p. 510.) It was from this letter by J.
Mitchell that Dr. Furnivall quoted in his article, Academy,
1882, vol. xxi. p. 269. See Furnivall above.]
