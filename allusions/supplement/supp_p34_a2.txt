[p. 146] In England the first recognition of Dante is by Chaucer in
the "Hugelin of Pisa" of the "Monkes Tale,"1 and an
invitation of the opening verses of the third canto of the
Inferno ("Assembly of Foules").
1 It is worth notice, as a proof of Chaucer's critical judgment, that he calls
Dante "the great poet of Itaille," while in the "Clerke’s Tale" he speaks of
Petrarch as a "wurthy clerk," as "the laureat poete" (alluding to the somewhat
sentimental ceremony at Rome), and says that his
"Rhetorike sweete
Enlumined all Itaille of poetry."
