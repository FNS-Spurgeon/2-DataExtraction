[p. 92] So far as it is possible to form an opinion, Chaucer achieved,
while living, a more than average share of social consideration
and eminence. He married well, was a man of business and
of the world, and had a keen enjoyment of existence. But
let us not put ourselves in the situation of his contemporaries,
and suppose that he was held by them in anything drawing
near to the esteem in which we hold him, as the father of our
poetry.
[p. 93] The time is not so far distant when we were unable to
distinguish the difference between Congreve and Shakespeare,
or, if we did, awarded the palm to the more modern and
legible poet. It was after Congreve's day that people set to
thinking what authors lived before Pope, and whether the
Canterbury Tales were worth perusing in any English but
Dryden's. It is not so much to boast of that Chaucer's fame
stands at last built on indestructible foundations, and that we
have learned to dispense with the service of his candle¬
holder! ...
