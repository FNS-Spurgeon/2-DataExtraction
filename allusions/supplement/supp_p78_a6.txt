1880. Hamilton, Walter. 'The Origin of the Office of Poet Laureate'
[in] Transactions of the Royal Historical Society, vol. viii, pp. 20,
30, 33, 35.
[p. 20] From the appointment of Chaucer about five hundred years
have elapsed, and during that period a long line of poets have
held the title of Laureate. ...
[p. 30] Geoffrey Chaucer, the first poet of any eminence who wrote
in the mother tongue, was styled Poet Laureate by his con¬
temporaries, and received several royal grants and offices. He
died on the 25th October, 1400, when his friend Sir John
Gower ... appears to have obtained or to have assumed the
title. . . .
p. 33] 1368. Edward III grants a daily pitcher of wine to Geoffrey
Chaucer, to be charged on the Port of London. Shortly after
his accession Richard II commuted that allowance for an
annual payment of twenty marks.
