[1595-6?] C[arew], R[ichard]. The Excellencie of the English tongue,
by R. C. of Anthony Esquire to W. C. Inserted by William
Camden in the 2nd edn. of his Remaines concerning Britaine, 1614,
pp. 43-4. (Elizabethan Critical Essays, ed. G. Gregory Smith,
1904, vol. ii, p. 293).
Adde hereunto, that whatsoeuer grace any other language
carrieth in verse or Prose, in Tropes or Metaphores, in Ecchoes
and Agnominations, they may all bee liuely and exactly
represented in ours : will you haue Platoes veine? reade Sir
Thomas Smith, the Ionicke? Sir Thomas Moore. Ciceroes?
Ascham, Varro, Chaucer, Demosthenes? Sir John Cheeks (who
in his treatise to the Rebels, hath comprised all the figures of
Rhetorick). Will you reade Virgill? take the Earle of Surrey,
Catullus? Shakespheare and Marlows [printed Barlows] frag¬
ment, Ovid? Daniell, Lucan? Spencer, Martial? Sir John
Davies and others: will you have all in all for Prose and
verse? Take the miracle of our age, Sir Philip Sidney.
