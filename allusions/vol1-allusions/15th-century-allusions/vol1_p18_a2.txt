[c. 1407.] Scogan, Henry. A moral balade made by Henry Scogane
squyer [addressed to Henry IV's sons], stanzas 9, 13, 17. MS.
Ashmole 59, ff. 26-27. Printed by Caxton [1478?], Caxton frag¬
ments, no. 1 [B. M. pr. mk. IB 55016), and by W. Thynne in
Chaucer’s works, 1532, ff. 380-1. (Chaucerian and other pieces, ed.
W. W. Skeat, 1897, pp. 239-40; Parallel text of Chaucer’s minor
poems, ed. F. J. Furnivall, Chaucer soc. [1871], pp. 427, 430.)
[fol. 26]
My maistre Chaucier / god his soule have /
þat in his langage / was so curyous
He saide þat be fader / nowe dede and grave /
Beqwaþe no-thing / his vertue with his hous /
Vn-to his sone /
(13)
[fol. 26b] By avncetrye þus may yee no-thing clayme
As þat my maistre Chaucier doþe expresse
But temporell thinge / þat man may hurte & mayme
þane is gode stocke / of vertuous noblesse.
..... herke howe my maistre seyde
[Here follow the three verses of Gentilesse.]
(17)
[fol. 27] Loo here þis noble Poete of Brettayne
Howe hyely he in vertuous sentence
þe lesse in youþe / of vertue / can compleyne
Wherfore I prey yowe / dooþe youre diligence
[Scogan quotes Chaucer's poem Gentilesse in full, as it is given in Ashmole MS.59,
fol. 27.]
