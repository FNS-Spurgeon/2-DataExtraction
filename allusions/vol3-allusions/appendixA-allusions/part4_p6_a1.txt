[c. 1460.] Burgh, Benedict. Translation of Cato’s Disticha Moralia,
stanza 41. [MS. Harl. 4733, fol. 9b.]
The lymytour þat vysiteth the wyfys
Ys wyse y-nough of hym a man may lere
To ȝeue gnidelis [needles] pynnys and knyuys
This craff is good thys doth the sely frere
Ȝeuith thyngys smale for thyngys þat bene dere
Ȝif thu resceyue ȝeue ay sumwhat agayne
[According to Caxton’s prologue to his own translatlon of Cato, 1483, Burgh made
And that wull noryssh frendys dere sertayne.
his for William, Viscount Bourchier; the latter was probably not born much before
1435, and was married in 1466. It is likely that this translation was made for him
during his youth. The reference is to Prol., ll. 233-4.]
