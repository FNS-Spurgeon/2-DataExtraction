[The Father-Poet appears to be sojourning in some country
town which he is unable to leave for want of money.]
I. To you, my Purse, and to no other wight,
Complain I, for thow art my lady dear;
I'm very sorry now that thow art light,
For certès thow dost make me heavy cheer,
I were as lief laid out upon my bier;
For which, unto your mercy thus I cry—
Be heavy again! or else I needs must die.

Scarcely any part of Chaucer's writings would raise a laugh
at the present day, though they might a blush. ... The
antiquated language in which his works are written prevents
our now appreciating much of the humour they contained. ...
No doubt at the time he was thought witty, and his tendency
in this direction is shown by his praise of mirth in the
'Romaunt of the Rose.'
